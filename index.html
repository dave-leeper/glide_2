Origins
The original version of the Glide Oracle was developed by Diana Slattery, PhD. It was modeled after the I Ching using symbols made from a deconstucted yin and yang symbol. Slatter refered to these symbols as a languange. It was claimed the symbols were recieved from aliens. The interpretation of the symbols' meaning seemed to be based off of a stream of consciousness impression made by each symbol.
Version 2.0 reinterpretes the symbols based on Daoism, which is where the yin and yang symbol is from. This reinterpretation consists of basing the 27 possible trigrams on existing Daoist principles. These 27 trigrams are then combined to form 729 hexagrams. The meanings of these hexagrams are built from the meanings of the two trigrams they contain. The top trigram represents external influences, while the bottom trigram represents internal influences.

The Icosiheptagon and Icosiheptagram created from the 27 trigrams are shown below, along with lines connecting each point. Each line represents two hexagrams. Each of the 27 points represents one hexagram.

Dao
What is the Dao? Is it God? Is it an unknowable supernatural force? Is it a principle? Is it a mystical state of mind? For the purposes of Glide 2.0, the Dao is that which causes change. We can place the Dao on a circle. In Eastern traditions it's common to draw the circle unclosed, in Western traditions it is closed. Either is acceptable.
Vitality, Energy, and Spirit
In Daoism, Vitality (精, Jīng), Energy (气, Qi), and Spirit (神, Shén) play an important role. In the book Vitality, Energy, Spirit, Thomas Cleary writes:
Vitality is primally associated with creativeity, temporally associated with sexuality. Energy is primally associated with movement, heat, and power, temporally associated with breath, magnetism, and strength. Spirit is primally associated with the essence of mind and consciousness, temporally associated with thought and reflection. As the three centers interact, the quality and proportion of their mutual influence relate directly to the total state of mental and physical well-being.

Much of Glide 2.0 concerns itself with these three facets of Daoism. Each gets its own trigram. Additionally, there are three groups of these forces interacting with one another. These groupings form unicursal hexagrams, known as the Above, In, and Below Unicursal Hexagrams.

Vitality, Sense, Spirit, Essense, And Will
Vitality, Sense, Spirit, Essense, And Will correspond to the ancient Chinese concepts of Water, Metal, Fire, Wood, and Earth of the Wu Xing. Wu Xing translates to "Five Movements" and Water, Metal, Fire, Wood, and Earth represent processes of nature. In the Wu Xing system, each of the movements fosters another movement and each hinders another movement. The Five Movements are an important part of Glide 2.0.

Vitality and Spirit were discussed above. Sense represents information from the five senses. It is closely related to Vitality, as both concern the body. Essense represents our mental selves. It is closely related to Spirit, as both concern the mind. Will represents willpower. Basically, Vitality, Energy, and Spirit represent three types of energies possessed by the body, Sense and Essense are produced by the body's normal operation, and Will drives the individual.

Wu Wei, translated literally, means not doing. This is usually interpreted as using the natural flow of events to accomplish tasks. Uncarved Block comes from the Chinese word Pu, meaning simplicity or uncarved wood. As used in Glide 2.0, it represents a person who practices Wu Wei and whose Vitality, Sense, Spirit, Essense, And Will are balanced.

The Uncarved Block, Wu Wei, and Energy are placed together on what is called the Triangle of Nature.
Story Telling and Character Arcs
Story Telling and Character Arcs Using the Unicursal Hexagrams
The Above, In, and Below Unicursal Hexagrams can be used as the basis for devoping stories and character arcs. This is done by tracing along the green or black arrows. Green arrows show the paths which nurture the following entry, whereas black arrows weaken the entry they point to. Here are a few of the stories/character arcs in the example hexagram to the right:
  • Killing → Exploitation → Forget
  • Killing → Exploitation → Forget → Fairness → Celebration → Craving → Killing
  • Forget → Craving → Fairness
  • Forget → Craving → Fairness → Exploitation → Celebration → Killing
  • Fairness → Celebration → Killing
An example story for the path of Killing → Exploitation → Forget → Fairness → Celebration → Craving → Killing could be an evil overloard killing his people (Killing). He exploits them ruthlessly, to the point they forget they ever lived another way (Forget). But a hero arises, looking to restore dignity to his people (Fairness). The people celebrate him and long for him to free them (Celebration, Craving). The hero kills the evil overload (Killing). Notice that in this example, each entry is nurtured because we are following the green arrows.

The path of Forget → Craving → Fairness → Exploitation → Celebration → Killing somewhat parallels the story of India under British rule and the rise of Mahatma Gandhi. The people have forgotten their once great past, and lost the craving for self rule (Forget, Craving). Gandhi arises, facing opposition (Fairness) he ends the exploitation (Exploitation), but there is no celebration (Celebration), only an end to the destruction of his people (Killing). Notice that in this example, each entry is weakened because we are following the black arrows.


Story Telling and Character Arcs Using the Wu Xing


The Wu Xing Pentagram can be used as the basis for devoping stories and character arcs. This is done by tracing along the green or black arrows. Green arrows show the paths which nurture the following entry, whereas black arrows weaken the entry they point to. Here are a few of the stories/character arcs in the example pentagram to the right:
  • Beginningless → Ultimately Free → Director
  • Beginningless → Ultimately Free → Director → Shadow Play → Stabilitze
  • Beginningless → Director → Stabilitze
  • Beginningless → Director → Stabilitze → Ultimately Free → Shadow Play
  • Director → Stabilitze → Beginningless
An example story for the path of Beginningless → Ultimately Free → Director → Shadow Play → Stabilitze could be the act of invention. There is the need for a new system in order to counter some problem. Looking at the situation, we see the need has always been there, but never recognized until now (Beginningless). After some time, our protagonist becomes has the power to enact a solution (Ultimately Free). The protagonost begins organizing things in order to enact the solution (Director). A prototype of the solution is enacted in order to discover any flaws in the idea (Shadow Play). The flaws are corrected and the solution implimented (Stabilize). Notice that in this example, each entry is nurtured because we are following the green arrows.

The path of Beginningless → Director → Stabilitze → Ultimately Free → Shadow Play tells a similar story, but one that goes wrong. There is the need for a new system in order to counter some problem. Looking at the situation, we see the need has always been there, but never recognized until now (Beginningless). The protagonost begins organizing things in an attempt to create a the solution (Director), but this time is lacking the inspiration found in the previous story, so the direction is aimless and haphazard. The protagonost attempts to fix the problems that arise (Stabilize) but the effort is hampered due to the fact it's unclear what the solution should be. An attempt to course correct to the right path is made (Ultimately Free), but is weakened due to the fact this attempt is based on the previous misguided actions rather than the original need. A final product is cobled together, but is only a shadow of what is actually needed (Shadow Play). Notice that in this example, each entry is weakened because we are following the black arrows.
Future Work
The three unicursal hexagrams of the trigrams (Above, In, and Below) can have their points placed on the points of an octadecagon. This octadecagon can further be dissected into 144 rhombi parallelograms, providing a home for the 144 hexagrams of the Zodiac I Ching. Each of the Zodiac I Ching trigrams can interact with the Glide 2.0 trigrams to form a hexagram. This will be examined in more detail in a future update.
