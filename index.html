The trigrams Spirit Above Energy, Energy Above Spirit, Spirit Above Vitality, Vitality Above Spirit, Energy Above Vitality, and and Vitality Above Energy can all be placed on a hexagram, called the Above Unicursal Hexagram. The trigrams Spirit In Energy, Energy In Spirit, Spirit In Vitality, Vitality In Spirit, Energy In Vitality, and Vitality In Energy can all be placed on a hexagram, called the In Unicursal Hexagram. The trigrams Spirit Below Energy, Energy Below Spirit, Spirit Below Vitality, Vitality Below Spirit, Energy Below Vitality, and Vitality Below Energy can all be placed on a hexagram, called the Below Unicursal Hexagram.

As you trace along the outside of these hexagrams (green arrows in the diagram) each trigram nurtures the one that follows it. As you trace along the hexagram (black arrows in the diagram) each trigram weakens the one that follows it.

This same nurturing/weakening relationship applies to the Wu Xing pentagram. Hexagrams which have an internal (bottom) trigram of one of these trigrams also follow these nurturing/weakening relationships.

Uncarved Block
The Uncarved Block represents natural development, Wu Wei represents natural action, and Energy is the energy that powers them both. These three trigrams can be placed together on what is called the Triangle of Nature.

With the Above Unicursal Hexagram, In Unicursal Hexagram, Below Unicursal Hexagram, Wu Xing pentagram, and the Triangle of Nature, all the trigrams except Dao have been assigned a shape. For completeness, we can place the Dao on a circle. In Eastern traditions it's common to draw the circle unclosed, in Western traditions it is closed. Either is acceptable.
The three unicursal hexagrams of the trigrams (Above, In, and Below) can have their points placed on the points of an octadecagon. This octadecagon can further be dissected into 144 rhombi parallelograms, providing a home for the 144 hexagrams of the Zodiac I Ching. Each of the Zodiac I Ching trigrams can interact with the Glide 2.0 trigrams to form a hexagram. This will be examined in more detail in a future update.

Origins
The original version of the Glide Oracle was developed by Diana Slattery. It was modeled after the I Ching using symbols made from a deconstucted yin and yang symbol. Slatter refered to these symbols as a languange. It was claimed the symbols were recieved from aliens. The interpretation of the symbols' meaning seemed to be based off of a stream of consciousness impression made by each symbol.
Version 2.0 reinterpretes the symbols based on Daoism, which is where the yin and yang symbol is from. This reinterpretation consists of basing the 27 possible trigrams on existing Daoist principles. These 27 trigrams are then combined to form 729 hexagrams. The meanings of these hexagrams are built from the meanings of the two trigrams they contain. The top trigram represents external influences, while the bottom trigram represents internal influences.
Vitality, Energy, and Spirit
In Daoism, Vitality, Energy, and Spirit play an important role. In the book Vitality, Energy, Spirit, Thomas Cleary writes:
Vitality is primally associated with creativeity, temporally associated with sexuality. Energy is primally associated with movement, heat, and power, temporally associated with breath, magnetism, and strength. Spirit is primally associated with the essence of mind and consciousness, temporally associated with thought and reflection. As the three centers interact, the quality and proportion of their mutual influence relate directly to the total state of mental and physical well-being.

Much of Glide 2.0 concerns itself with these three facets of Daoism.

Story Telling and Character Arcs Using the Unicursal Hexagrams
Vitality, Sense, Spirit, Essense, And Will
Vitality, Sense, Spirit, Essense, And Will correspond to the ancient Chinese concepts of Water, Metal, Fire, Wood, and Earth of the Wu Xing. Wu Xing translates to "Five Movements" and Water, Metal, Fire, Wood, and Earth represent processes of nature. In the Wu Xing system, each of the movements fosters another movement and each hinders another movement. The Five Movements are an important part of Glide 2.0.

Vitality and Spirit were discussed above. Sense represents information from the five senses. It is closely related to Vitality, as both concern the body. Essense represents our mental selves. It is closely related to Spirit, as both concern the mind. Will represents willpower. Basically, Vitality, Energy, and Spirit represent three types of energies possessed by the body, Sense and Essense are produced by the body's normal operation, and Will drives the individual.

See the Shapes section for additional information about how Wu Xing is used in Glide 2.0.
Wu Wei And Uncarved Block
Wu Wei, translated literally, means not doing. This is usually interpreted as using the natural flow of events to accomplish tasks.

Uncarved Block comes from the Chinese word Pu, meaning simplicity or uncarved wood. As used in Glide 2.0, it represents a person who practices Wu Wei and whose Vitality, Sense, Spirit, Essense, And Will are balanced.
